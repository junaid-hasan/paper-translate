<html><body><h1 data-bbox="437 28 562 56">EXPOSÉ I</h1><h1 data-bbox="307 99 692 130">MORPHISMES ÉTALES</h1><p data-bbox="59 220 940 279">To simplify the exposition, we suppose that all preschemes under consideration are locally noetherian, at least after number 2.</p><h2 data-bbox="59 300 480 330">1. Notions of differential calculus</h2><p data-bbox="59 350 940 709">Let $X$ be a prescheme over $Y$, let $\Delta_{X/Y}$ or $\Delta$ be the diagonal morphism $X \to X \times_Y X$. It is an immersion morphism, hence a closed immersion morphism of $X$ into an open $V$ of $X \times_Y X$. Let $\mathcal{I}_X$ be the ideal of the closed subscheme corresponding to the diagonal in $V$ (N.B. if one wishes to do things intrinsically, without assuming $X$ separated over $Y$ — hypothesis which would be canular — one should consider the inverse image ensemble of $\mathcal{O}_{X \times X}$ in $X$, and designate by $\mathcal{I}_X$ the ideal of augmentation in the latter...). The sheaf $\mathcal{I}_X / \mathcal{I}_X^2$ can be regarded as a quasi-coherent sheaf on $X$, denoted by $\Omega_{X/Y}^1$. It is of finite type if $X \to Y$ is of finite type. It behaves well with respect to extension of the base $Y' \to Y$. We also introduce the sheaves $\mathcal{O}_{X \times_Y X} / \mathcal{I}_X^{n+1} = \mathcal{P}_X^n/Y$, which are sheaves of rings on $X$, making $X$ a prescheme which one can denote by $\Delta_{X/Y}^n$ and call the $n$-th infinitesimal neighborhood of $X/Y$. The outcome is of total triviality, even though it is rather long (1); it would be prudent to speak of it only at a moment when one says something useful, with smooth morphisms.</p><h2 data-bbox="59 730 480 760">2. Quasi-finite morphisms</h2><p data-bbox="59 780 940 840"><strong>Proposition 2.1.</strong> — Let $A \to B$ be a local homomorphism (N.B. the rings are now noetherian), $\mathfrak{m}$ the maximal ideal of $A$. Equivalent conditions:</p><div class="formula" data-bbox="59 850 940 900"><p>(i) $B/\mathfrak{m}B$ is of finite dimension over $k = A/\mathfrak{m}$.</p><p>(ii) $\mathfrak{m}B$ is a defining ideal, and $B/\mathfrak{r}(B) = k(B)$ is an extension of $k = k(A)$</p><p>(iii) The completion $\hat{B}$ is finite over that of $\hat{A}$ of $A$.</p></div><p data-bbox="59 930 270 950">(1) cf. EGA IV 16.3.</p></body></html>
